The three losing patterns

When we compared the 6 worst ads to the 6 best, three sins repeat.

Loser pattern #1

Question hooks don't close the sale

2 of 6

Bottom-cohort ads opened with a question (33% of losers) while zero winners did. Questions invite thinking. The feed doesn't reward thinking — it rewards stopping.

"The ad uses 'problem bombing', listing 6 distinct issues in the first 17 seconds."— Gemini insight, loser cohort

Loser pattern #2

Storytime narration delays the reveal

2 of 6

Losers used 3rd-person narration (33% vs ~0% in winners) and one waited until 87% of the video to even reveal the product. First-person ownership of the problem converts; detached storytelling does not.

"Each mushroom is paired with a specific benefit and a corresponding lifestyle visual."— Gemini insight, loser cohort

Loser pattern #3

Scarcity CTA without a clear next step

no reversal

Worst performers leaned on urgency ("sale ending", "free gifts") without pairing it with a risk-reversal like a guarantee. Scarcity without safety reads as pressure — and pressure bounces.

"Urgency is manufactured at the end by mentioning a sale ending and high demand."— Gemini insight, loser cohort

Loser pattern #4

Too many faces in frame

-32%

Losers had 1.8 people on screen at max, vs 1.2 for winners. The winning format is intimate: one creator, looking into the camera, talking to you.

The three winning patterns

What the top 6 did that the others didn't.

Winner pattern #1

22% more cuts per minute

35 cuts/min

Winners cut every 1.7 seconds on average (35/min vs 27/min for losers). Anatomical animations, b-roll inserts, and text overlays acted as pattern interrupts that reset attention before boredom set in.

"High visual variety with 38 cuts per minute prevents viewer fatigue in a long-form ad."— Gemini insight, winner cohort

Winner pattern #2

Risk-reversal, not urgency

money-back

Every top-performer closed with a 30-day money-back guarantee framed as "Risk-Free". Winners make buying safe; losers make it scary. Both reduce friction — but safety converts.

"The final 15 seconds are dedicated to risk reversal (money-back guarantee)."— Gemini insight, winner cohort

Winner pattern #3

"Learn from my mistakes" framing

peer, not pitch

Top ads pivoted at ~8 seconds from "here's a problem" to "here's what I got wrong". A debunking beat + failed-solutions list positions the creator as a peer — not a salesperson. It dismantles defences before the product reveal.

"The ad uses a 'debunking' beat to build trust before introducing the product."— Gemini insight, winner cohort

Winner pattern #4

Bold claims beat curiosity gaps

0 questions

Winners led with bold claims, pattern interrupts, and visual reveals. None used question hooks. In a vertical feed, certainty is a differentiator — doubt is a swipe.

The Ryze playbook

What to do more of

1

Open with certainty, not curiosity

Bold claim, visual reveal, or pattern interrupt in the first 5 seconds. No question hooks. No "wait for it".

2

Cut every ~1.7 seconds

Target 35+ cuts per minute. Use anatomical animations, text overlays, b-roll inserts — anything that resets attention without changing the voice.

3

First-person, one face

A single creator looking straight into the camera, narrating their own experience. Two people on screen is already too many.

4

Pivot to peer at ~8 seconds

After the hook: "I used to think X too" or "Here's what I got wrong". Turn off the selling, turn on the teaching.

5

Close with risk reversal, not urgency

30-day money-back guarantee, framed as "Risk-Free". Skip the countdown timers and stock-running-out language.

What to cut from the queue

✕

Question hooks

"Why does your stomach feel bloated?" invites the viewer to think — and then swipe. Swap to a bold claim.

✕

3rd-person narration

"Ancient healers believed…" is a podcast intro, not an ad. First-person or nothing.

✕

Storytime with delayed product reveal

If the product doesn't appear until the second half, you've already lost 70% of viewers. Tease the payoff in the first 10 seconds.

✕

Pure scarcity CTAs

"Sale ends tonight" without a safety net reads as pressure. Pair urgency with a guarantee or drop it.

Appendix

Method · how winner/loser cohorts are defined

Of 36 videos scored by video-analyzer v2 (Gemini, temperature 0.15, seed 42), 26 matched rows in matched_rows.csv. Of those, 24 had at least $50 in spend — ads below that threshold are statistically unreliable. Winners are the top 6 by spend-weighted ROAS; losers the bottom 6. Deltas and categorical splits are computed inside those cohorts only.

Correlations at n≈24 are exploratory, not causal. Use the findings to generate hypotheses for next week's test batch, not to fire a creator.
